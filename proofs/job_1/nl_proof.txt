I'll interpret "b2" as b² and make the missing hypothesis explicit: this is the Pythagorean theorem for a Euclidean right triangle, with a,b the legs and c the hypotenuse.

Theorem. Let ABC be a Euclidean right triangle with right angle at C. Let BC=a, AC=b, AB=c, so that a and b are the legs and c is the hypotenuse. Then a²+b²=c².

The statement needs this right-triangle hypothesis. For arbitrary numbers a,b,c, the equation is false; for example, a=b=c=1 gives 1²+1²≠1².

Proof Sketch

We prove the theorem using similar triangles, one of the classical Euclidean proofs of Pythagoras.

Let ABC be a right triangle with ∠C=90°. Drop the altitude from C to the hypotenuse AB. Let this altitude meet AB at D.

Thus D lies on AB, and CD⊥AB.

Let AD=x, DB=y. Since AB=c, we have x+y=c.

The key idea is that the altitude to the hypotenuse decomposes the original right triangle into two smaller right triangles, each similar to the original triangle.

Step 1: Show △ACD∼△ABC

Both triangles have a right angle: ∠ADC=90°, ∠ACB=90°. They also share the angle at A: ∠CAD=∠CAB. Therefore, by the AA similarity criterion, △ACD∼△ABC.

Now compare corresponding sides. In the similarity △ACD∼△ABC, the side AC=b corresponds to AB=c, and the side AD=x corresponds to AC=b. Hence AC/AD = AB/AC. Substituting the side lengths gives b/x = c/b. Multiplying both sides by bc, we obtain b²=cx.

So the square on the leg b equals the product of the hypotenuse c and the hypotenuse segment x=AD.

Step 2: Show △BCD∼△ABC

Similarly, both triangles have a right angle: ∠BDC=90°, ∠ACB=90°. They also share the angle at B: ∠CBD=∠CBA. Thus, again by AA similarity, △BCD∼△ABC.

In this similarity, BC=a corresponds to AB=c, and DB=y corresponds to BC=a. Therefore, BC/DB = AB/BC. Substituting the side lengths gives a/y = c/a. Multiplying both sides by ac, we get a²=cy.

So the square on the leg a equals the product of the hypotenuse c and the hypotenuse segment y=DB.

Step 3: Add the two identities

We have proved a²=cy and b²=cx. Adding these equations yields a²+b²=cy+cx. Factor out c: a²+b²=c(x+y). But x+y=AD+DB=AB=c. Hence a²+b²=c·c=c².

Therefore, a²+b²=c².

This proves the Pythagorean theorem.

Key Lemmas and Techniques Used

The proof relies on two standard Euclidean facts:
1. AA similarity criterion: If two triangles have two equal angles, then they are similar.
2. Altitude-to-hypotenuse similarity: In a right triangle, the altitude from the right angle to the hypotenuse creates two smaller right triangles, each similar to the original triangle.

The main proof strategy is to convert geometric similarity into algebraic identities: a²=cy, b²=cx, then add them and use x+y=c.

## Final Verdict

VERDICT: PROVED — Under the standard Pythagorean interpretation—that a and b are the legs of a Euclidean right triangle and c is its hypotenuse—the conjecture a²+b²=c² is proved.
