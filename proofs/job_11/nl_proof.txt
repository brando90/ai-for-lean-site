# Review and Improvement of the Proof Sketch

I agree with the reviewer's central conclusion: **the conjecture is false as stated**, and the reviewer's counterexample is fundamentally correct. Below I re-examine the argument, tighten a few details, flag one small edge case, and clarify what would actually need to be fixed before a square-root law could be sought.

---

## 1. Restating the obstruction

The Bonacich centrality is only defined when `β < ρ(A_S)^{-1}`, hence admissibility of a subset `S` is part of the constraint. Write
$$
\mathcal{A} := \{S \subseteq [n] : \beta \rho(A_S) < 1\}
$$
for the collection of admissible subsets. The conjecture defines
$$
S^*(u) = \arg\max_{\,S\in\mathcal{A}} \{|S| : b_i(\beta,S)\geq u \;\; \forall i\in S\}.
$$
For this to be well-defined, one needs:
(a) the family `\mathcal{A}` to be closed under suprema (or at least the subfamily of "good" sets to have a maximum), or
(b) a deterministic tie-breaking rule.

Neither holds on `G(n,c/n)`. The obstruction is already visible on a **fixed** finite tree that appears linearly often with high probability.

---

## 2. The double-star counterexample (verified and slightly sharpened)

Fix `0<\beta<1/c` (in particular `\beta<1` since `c>1`). Choose
$$
d := \left\lfloor \beta^{-2} \right\rfloor, \qquad\text{so that}\qquad \beta^2 d \leq 1 \leq \beta^2(d+1).
$$
*(Using the floor rather than `⌈β^{-2}⌉-1` avoids the degenerate case `β^{-2}\in\mathbb{Z}`; if `β^{-2}` is an integer one can perturb `β` by an arbitrarily small amount since the hypothesis `β<1/c` is open.)*

Let `H_d` be the double-star with adjacent centres `x,y`, each having `d` pendant leaves. Define
$$
S_x = \{x\}\cup N_{H_d}(x)\setminus\{y\}, \qquad S_y = \{y\}\cup N_{H_d}(y)\setminus\{x\}.
$$
Each induces `K_{1,d}`.

**Bonacich values on `K_{1,d}`.** The resolvent identity gives, on a star with center `c` and `d` leaves,
$$
b_c = \frac{1+\beta d}{1-\beta^2 d},\qquad b_{\text{leaf}} = \frac{1+\beta}{1-\beta^2 d}.
$$
Since `0<\beta<1` and `β^2d<1`, both quantities are finite and strictly greater than `1`. The smaller is the leaf value, so put
$$
u_0 := \frac{1+\beta}{1-\beta^2 d} > 1.
$$
For every `u\in (1,u_0]`, the sets `S_x` and `S_y` are both admissible (since `ρ(K_{1,d})=\sqrt d<1/\beta`) and satisfy `b_i(\beta,S)\geq u` for all `i\in S`.

**Maximality.** I verify that no admissible proper superset of `S_x` (within `H_d`) satisfies the threshold:

- *Adding the second centre `y`.* The induced subgraph is `K_{1,d+1}`, with `ρ=\sqrt{d+1}\geq 1/\beta` by the choice of `d`. Hence it is **not** admissible. (For generic `β` the inequality is strict.)
- *Adding a leaf of `y` without adding `y`.* That leaf is isolated in the induced subgraph, so its Bonacich value equals `1<u`. Threshold fails.
- *Any larger set containing both additions.* Contains `K_{1,d+1}` as a subgraph, so `ρ\geq\sqrt{d+1}\geq 1/\beta` by Perron-Frobenius subgraph monotonicity — not admissible.

By symmetry the same holds for `S_y`. Thus `S_x` and `S_y` are **two distinct incomparable maximal** admissible threshold-satisfying subsets of `V(H_d)`, and `S_x\cup S_y=V(H_d)` has `ρ>1/\beta` (its spectral radius is `\tfrac{1+\sqrt{1+4d}}{2}>\sqrt{d+1}`), so is not admissible. There is **no** admissible fixed point of `T_u` containing both.

---

## 3. Occurrence of `H_d` as an isolated component in `G(n,c/n)`

`H_d` is a tree with `k=2d+2` vertices and `k-1` edges and bounded automorphism group. Standard first/second-moment computation gives
$$
\mathbb{E}[\#\text{ isolated copies of }H_d \text{ in }G(n,c/n)] \;=\; \frac{n^{k}}{|\mathrm{Aut}(H_d)|}\Bigl(\frac{c}{n}\Bigr)^{k-1}(1-c/n)^{\binom{k}{2}-(k-1)}(1-c/n)^{k(n-k)} \;=\; \Theta(n).
$$
The variance is also `\Theta(n)` (via Chen-Stein coupling for tree-component counts, see e.g. Janson–Łuczak–Ruciński, *Random Graphs*, §3-4), so the number of isolated `H_d`-components concentrates and is positive with probability tending to `1`.

---

## 4. The global failure

Bonacich centrality is block-diagonal in the connected components of the induced subgraph `G[S]`: `(I-\beta A_S)^{-1}\mathbf{1}` decomposes componentwise. Consequently, if `H_d` is an isolated component of `G`, then for any global candidate `S`, its restriction to `V(H_d)` is an admissible `u`-threshold set of `H_d`. Any "global largest" set would be forced to pick either `S_x` or `S_y` on that component, but not both; swapping the choice gives another admissible candidate not contained in the first. Hence the conjectured `S^*(u)` **does not exist** as a largest set, for every `u\in(1,u_0]`, with probability `1-o(1)`.

This failure is *independent* of the claimed phase transition at `u_c`: it occurs for an interval of thresholds `u` only slightly above `1`, regardless of whether a critical scaling exists.

---

## 5. Minor corrections/improvements to the original sketch

1. **Edge case for `β^{-2}\in\mathbb{Z}`.** The reviewer sets `d=⌈β^{-2}⌉-1`, which yields equality `β^2(d+1)=1` exactly at integer `β^{-2}` (e.g. `β=1/2`). There `ρ(K_{1,d+1})=1/\beta`, and the definition requires `β<ρ^{-1}` strictly, so the argument is *still* correct, but using `d=\lfloor\beta^{-2}\rfloor` plus an infinitesimal perturbation of `β` makes the dominant inequality strict and cleaner.

2. **"Spectral warning sign."** The reviewer's remark that `[n]` itself fails admissibility is valid but inessential; the definition formally requires `β<ρ(A_{S^*(u)})^{-1}`, not `β<ρ(A_{[n]})^{-1}`. The real problem is not starting-point admissibility but *joinability* of admissible fixed points (i.e., failure of the Knaster-Tarski conclusion on the non-lattice `\mathcal{A}`).

3. **Knaster-Tarski.** `T_u` is monotone (Bonacich centrality is monotone under induced-subgraph inclusion), but admissibility is **not** closed under union — `S_x,S_y\in\mathcal{A}` while `S_x\cup S_y\notin\mathcal{A}`. This is precisely why the "maximal fixed point" language in the conjecture is inconsistent on `G(n,c/n)`.

4. **Verifying incomparability carefully.** `S_x\cap S_y=\varnothing`, yet `S_x\cup S_y=V(H_d)` is not admissible. So the "largest admissible threshold-satisfying subset of `V(H_d)`" simply does not exist.

---

## 6. How the conjecture could be rescued

Any serious attempt should:

1. **Repair the definition.** Either (i) fix a deterministic canonical rule among maximal admissible fixed points (e.g., lexicographic, or the one maximising `\sum_i b_i`); or (ii) reformulate `S^*(u)` purely through the cavity/message-passing fixed point on the Poisson(`c`) Galton-Watson local limit, using the recursion
$$
B \stackrel{d}{=} 1 + \beta\sum_{j=1}^{\mathrm{Poi}(c)} B_j\cdot \mathbf{1}\{B_j\geq u\},
$$
and let `\phi^*(u)=\mathbb{P}(B\geq u)`.

2. **Prove existence of a nondegenerate branching fixed point.** Under `\beta c<1` the linear operator `\beta c` has spectral radius `<1`, so small-`u` iterates contract; a saddle-node bifurcation in `u` then yields `u_c`.

3. **Derive the square-root scaling.** A generic saddle-node of the scalar map `F_u(\phi)=\phi` gives `\phi^*(u)-\phi_c\sim C\sqrt{u_c-u}`, provided `\partial_\phi^2 F\neq 0` at criticality (this is the Aldous–Pittel–Spencer–Wormald template for `k`-cores).

Such a programme is plausible but is **not** what the current statement says, and does not prove it.

---

## Final Verdict

## Final Verdict
VERDICT: DISPROVED — The conjecture is false as stated: for every fixed `0<β<1/c` there is an open interval `u\in(1,u_0]` on which `G(n,c/n)` almost surely contains an isolated double-star `H_d` exhibiting two incomparable maximal admissible threshold-satisfying subsets, so the purported "largest" set `S^*(u)` is undefined with high probability and the order parameter `\phi^*(u)` of parts (i)–(iii) is not well-posed.
