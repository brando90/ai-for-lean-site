Theorem: known smoothed shadow-simplex upper bounds

In the standard Gaussian smoothed model used in the simplex literature, there are shadow-vertex based simplex methods whose expected number of pivots is polynomial, and recent analyses give bounds of the form

O(σ^{-1/2} d^{11/4} log(M)^{7/4})

for a semi-random shadow-vertex method, up to lower-order terms and model conventions. Bach and Huiberts state this as an improvement over the 2023 bound O(σ^{-3/2} d^{13/4} log(M)^{7/4}), and also prove a matching noise-dependence lower bound for the relevant diameter/shadow-path geometry.

Proof sketch of the known upper-bound mechanism

Assume first that the perturbed instance is nondegenerate. This holds with probability one under independent Gaussian perturbations: degeneracy of a basis, equality of two competing ratio-test values, or nonunique optimality is described by the vanishing of a nonzero polynomial in the input coefficients, and a continuous Gaussian distribution assigns probability zero to such algebraic hypersurfaces. In the shadow-vertex analyses, this nondegeneracy is used to identify simplex pivots with vertices of a two-dimensional shadow polygon; Bach and Huiberts state the corresponding nondegeneracy fact for Gaussian matrices explicitly.

Let P = {x ∈ R^d : Ax ≤ b}.

The shadow-vertex rule is defined using two objective vectors y_0, y_1. It follows the path of vertices that are optimal for objectives of the form y_λ = (1-λ)y_0 + λy_1, 0 ≤ λ ≤ 1. Equivalently, one projects P orthogonally onto the two-dimensional subspace span(y_0, y_1). Each visited vertex of P projects to a vertex of the planar "shadow" polygon, so the number of pivots is bounded by the number of edges or vertices of that shadow polygon. This is the central reduction in the shadow-vertex method.

The analytic core is therefore a shadow-size bound. One must show that after Gaussian perturbation, no fixed or suitably independent two-dimensional projection can have too many vertices in expectation. The estimates proceed by controlling the probability that a given basis appears on the shadow path. Geometrically, a basis can be on the path only if its normal cone intersects the two-dimensional objective plane. Gaussian perturbation makes these normal cones unlikely to align pathologically with the plane. The modern proofs refine this idea using separation arguments for normal cones, Gaussian anti-concentration inequalities, and careful counting of bases that can simultaneously lie on the shadow boundary.
