# Improved Proof Sketch

## Setup and the Key Reduction

Let $L_n = \operatorname{lcm}(1,2,\ldots,n)$ and write the $n$th harmonic number in lowest terms as
$$
H_n = 1 + \tfrac12 + \cdots + \tfrac1n = \frac{u_n}{d_n}, \qquad \gcd(u_n,d_n)=1.
$$
The sequence $a_n$ is defined by $H_n = a_n/L_n$, so
$$
a_n = H_n L_n = \sum_{k=1}^{n} \frac{L_n}{k},
$$
which is an integer because $k \mid L_n$ for each $1 \le k \le n$.

**Lemma (Key Reduction).** $\gcd(a_n,L_n) = L_n/d_n.$

*Proof.* Since $d_n$ is the reduced denominator of $H_n = a_n/L_n$, we have $d_n \mid L_n$. Write $L_n = d_n c_n$ with $c_n = L_n/d_n \in \mathbb Z_{>0}$. Then
$$
a_n = H_n L_n = \frac{u_n}{d_n}\cdot d_n c_n = u_n c_n.
$$
Therefore
$$
\gcd(a_n, L_n) = \gcd(u_n c_n,\, d_n c_n) = c_n\cdot\gcd(u_n,d_n)=c_n = \frac{L_n}{d_n},
$$
using $\gcd(u_n,d_n) = 1.$ $\square$

So the conjecture is equivalent to the two statements:

- **(A)** $d_n = L_n$ for infinitely many $n$ (equivalent to $\gcd(a_n,L_n)=1$ i.o.).
- **(B)** $d_n < L_n$ for infinitely many $n$ (equivalent to $\gcd(a_n,L_n)>1$ i.o.).

## Proof of (B): $\gcd(a_n, L_n) > 1$ Infinitely Often

This is a $3$-adic argument (essentially a special case of the Kürschák / Eswarathasan–Levine philosophy). Let $v_3(\cdot)$ denote the $3$-adic valuation on $\mathbb Q^\times$.

Fix $k \ge 1$ and restrict to $n$ in the interval
$$
2\cdot 3^k \;\le\; n \;\le\; 3^{k+1}-1.
$$
These intervals are nonempty for every $k\ge 1$, and they are disjoint and pairwise tend to infinity, so they contain infinitely many integers $n$.

**Step 1: $v_3(L_n) = k$.** The highest power of $3$ that is at most $n$ is exactly $3^k$, since $3^{k+1}>n$. Thus $3^k \mid L_n$ but $3^{k+1} \nmid L_n$.

**Step 2: the only terms of $H_n$ with $v_3(1/j) = -k$.** Among $j \in \{1,\ldots,n\}$, the multiples of $3^k$ are precisely
$$
3^k \quad\text{and}\quad 2\cdot 3^k,
$$
because $3\cdot 3^k = 3^{k+1} > n$, and no multiple has a strictly larger power of $3$. Hence exactly two summands of $H_n$, namely $1/3^k$ and $1/(2\cdot 3^k)$, have $3$-adic valuation $-k$; every other summand $1/j$ satisfies $v_3(1/j) \ge -(k-1)$.

**Step 3: the two "bad" summands cancel a factor of $3$.** We compute
$$
\frac{1}{3^k} + \frac{1}{2\cdot 3^k} \;=\; \frac{2+1}{2\cdot 3^k} \;=\; \frac{3}{2\cdot 3^k} \;=\; \frac{1}{2\cdot 3^{k-1}},
$$
whose $3$-adic valuation is $-(k-1)$, not $-k$.

**Step 4: conclude $v_3(H_n) \ge -(k-1)$.** Write
$$
H_n \;=\; \underbrace{\left(\tfrac{1}{3^k}+\tfrac{1}{2\cdot 3^k}\right)}_{v_3 = -(k-1)} \;+\; \sum_{\substack{1\le j\le n\\ j\ne 3^k,\, 2\cdot 3^k}} \frac{1}{j}.
$$
Every term on the right has $v_3 \ge -(k-1)$. By the non-archimedean (ultrametric) inequality,
$$
v_3(H_n)\;\ge\; -(k-1).
$$
Therefore $v_3(d_n) = -v_3(H_n) \le k-1 < k = v_3(L_n)$, so
$$
3 \;\Big\vert\; \frac{L_n}{d_n} \;=\; \gcd(a_n,L_n).
$$
In particular $\gcd(a_n,L_n) \ge 3 > 1$.

This holds for every $n$ in $[2\cdot 3^k,\, 3^{k+1}-1]$ for every $k\ge 1$, yielding infinitely many $n$. (Indeed, the first few witnesses are $n\in\{6,7,8\}$, $\{18,\ldots,26\}$, $\{54,\ldots,80\}$, $\{162,\ldots,242\},\ldots$, e.g. $n=6$ gives $\gcd=3$ and $n=20$ gives $\gcd=15$.) $\square$

**Remark.** The same argument goes through with any odd prime $p$ in place of $3$, using the intervals $[2p^k,\, p^{k+1}-1]$. It also generalizes: whenever the set of multiples of $p^k$ in $\{1,\ldots,n\}$ can be partitioned into residue classes whose reciprocals sum to something $p$-adically small, one obtains $p \mid L_n/d_n$.

## Status of (A): $\gcd(a_n, L_n) = 1$ Infinitely Often

Numerically (A) is very plausible: $d_n = L_n$ holds for $n = 1,2,3,4,5,9,10,11,12,13,14,15,16,17,\ldots$ (all values where no "harmonic cancellation" at any prime occurs). Outside the intervals covered by arguments like the one above (for $p=3,5,7,\ldots$), the equality $d_n = L_n$ happens frequently.

However, turning this numerical abundance into a proof of **infinitude** requires showing: for infinitely many $n$, the $p$-adic valuation $v_p(H_n)$ equals $-v_p(L_n)$ **simultaneously for every prime $p \le n$**. No prime is allowed to "cancel" in the harmonic sum at such an $n$.

This is exactly the still-unresolved conjecture on harmonic denominators recorded by **Peter Shiu** in *The denominators of harmonic numbers* (arXiv:1607.02863). The expected answer is "yes" but there is no known proof. Heuristic arguments suggest the set $\{n : d_n = L_n\}$ has density $\prod_{p}(1 - \varepsilon_p) > 0$ for small "exception probabilities" $\varepsilon_p$ at each prime, but making any density estimate rigorous runs into delicate uniformity problems across infinitely many primes.

Because (A) is open, the full conjecture cannot be declared proved at this time.

## Critique of the Reviewed Sketch

- The reviewed sketch's logical structure — reducing to $\gcd(a_n,L_n) = L_n/d_n$, then handling (B) by a $3$-adic argument and pointing to Shiu's paper for (A) — is **correct and efficient**. I made three improvements:
  1. Made the divisibility $d_n \mid L_n$ explicit (not just asserted) by noting that $H_n = \sum_k L_n/k \,/\, L_n$ has integral numerator.
  2. Stated the ultrametric inequality explicitly when concluding $v_3(H_n) \ge -(k-1)$; the reviewed sketch was a little informal there.
  3. Added the remark that the same argument works for any odd prime $p$, giving many independent infinite families (the sketch's $3$-only version already suffices, but this is useful context).
- The reviewed sketch's sample values are correct.
- The reviewed sketch's citation of Shiu's paper for the open status of (A) matches the literature as I know it.
