## 1. The Product Formula for σ*(n)

Let `n = p_1^{a_1} p_2^{a_2} ⋯ p_r^{a_r}` be the prime factorization. A divisor `d` of `n` is *unitary* iff `gcd(d, n/d) = 1`. If `d = ∏ p_i^{e_i}` with `0 ≤ e_i ≤ a_i`, then `n/d = ∏ p_i^{a_i - e_i}`, and `gcd(d, n/d) = 1` requires that for every `i`, either `e_i = 0` or `a_i - e_i = 0`. Hence each prime-power block `p_i^{a_i}` is either entirely included in `d` or entirely excluded.

Consequently, the unitary divisors are in bijection with subsets of `{1, …, r}`, and
`σ*(n) = ∏_{i=1}^r (1 + p_i^{a_i})`.

The number `n` is **unitary perfect** iff `σ*(n) = 2n`, equivalently
`∏_{i=1}^r (1 + 1/p_i^{a_i}) = 2`. (★)

## 2. No Odd Unitary Perfect Number

Suppose `n` is odd and unitary perfect, with `r` distinct prime factors, all odd. For each `i`, `1 + p_i^{a_i}` is even (odd + odd), so `2^r ∣ σ*(n) = 2n`. Since `n` is odd, `v_2(2n) = 1`, forcing `r = 1`. Then `n = p^a` and (★) reads `1 + p^{-a} = 2`, i.e. `p^a = 1`, impossible. Hence every unitary perfect number is even.

(Minor correction to the reviewed sketch: this argument bounds `r ≤ 1`, then derives a contradiction; the logical flow is `2^r ∣ 2n` combined with `v_2(2n) = 1`.)

## 3. The Five Known Examples and the Status of the Conjecture

The known unitary perfect numbers are
`6, 60, 90, 87360, 146361946186458562560000`,
discovered by Subbarao–Warren (1966) and completed by Wall (1975), who showed the fifth is the next after `87360`. No sixth example is known as of OEIS A002827 (entry last revised 2026-04-24).

## 4. Finiteness for Each Fixed ω(n) (Conditional Result)

Let `ω(n) = r`. Equation (★) is a unit-fraction-type identity:
`∑ over subsets … = 2`, more precisely `∏ (1 + x_i) = 2` with `x_i = 1/p_i^{a_i}, 0 < x_i ≤ 1/2`.

**Lemma (Erdős-style finiteness for fixed `r`).** *For each fixed `r`, there are only finitely many `r`-tuples of prime powers `(p_1^{a_1}, …, p_r^{a_r})` with `∏(1 + p_i^{-a_i}) = 2`.*

*Proof sketch.* Order the primes `p_1 < p_2 < ⋯ < p_r`. From (★), `(1 + 1/p_1^{a_1}) ≥ 2^{1/r}`, so `p_1^{a_1} ≤ 1/(2^{1/r} - 1)`, a finite bound. Having fixed `p_1^{a_1}`, the residual identity for `(p_2, …, p_r)` is again of the same form with the new target `2/(1 + p_1^{-a_1})`, and `p_2^{a_2}` is bounded in terms of `r` and `p_1^{a_1}`. Inductively, every prime power is bounded by a function of `r` alone, so only finitely many tuples exist. ∎

This is the kind of bound made effective by Goto and others. It proves: *for each `r`, only finitely many unitary perfect numbers have exactly `r` prime factors.*

## 5. Why This Does Not Prove the Full Conjecture

The conjecture asks for **absolute** finiteness, which would follow if one could show that `r = ω(n)` is **uniformly** bounded over all unitary perfect numbers. This is the obstruction:

- The same Egyptian-fraction style equation `∑ 1/n_i = 1` (Sylvester–Fibonacci style) admits solutions with arbitrarily many terms; so the structural identity (★) does not by itself force `r` to be bounded.
- Wall (1988) proved that any new unitary perfect number must have at least **nine** odd prime-power components — a *lower* bound on complexity. No corresponding *upper* bound on `ω(n)` is known.
- Wall (1987) showed any new example has an odd component exceeding `2^15`.
- The classical analogue — the conjecture that there are only finitely many odd perfect numbers — is itself open, and even the boundedness of `ω(n)` for ordinary perfect numbers is unresolved (though here we have the much weaker fact that no odd unitary perfect number exists at all).

Thus the natural strategy collapses precisely at the step requiring uniform control of `ω(n)`.

## 6. Sharper Restrictions on a Hypothetical Sixth Example

Combining the known results (Subbarao–Warren 1966, Wall 1975/1987/1988):
1. `n` is even.
2. `ω(n) ≥ 10` (the factor of 2 plus ≥ 9 odd prime-power components).
3. The odd part of `n` has a prime-power component `> 2^{15}`.
4. `n > 10^{22}` at minimum, given the size of the fifth example.
5. The 2-adic valuation `v_2(n)` is constrained: writing `n = 2^a m` with `m` odd, `1 + 2^a` divides `2n = 2^{a+1} m`, so `1 + 2^a` is odd and divides `m`. Hence the odd part of `n` is divisible by `1 + 2^a`.

These all narrow the search but do not close it.

## 7. Summary of What Is and Isn't Proved

**Proved unconditionally:**
- The product formula `σ*(n) = ∏ (1 + p_i^{a_i})`.
- Every unitary perfect number is even.
- Finiteness of unitary perfect numbers with at most `r` prime factors, for each fixed `r`.
- Strong arithmetic obstructions to the existence of further examples.

**Not proved:**
- A uniform upper bound on `ω(n)` over all unitary perfect numbers.
- Hence absolute finiteness — the full conjecture.

The reviewed sketch's analysis is correct: the proof strategy reaches a wall at uniform control of `ω(n)`, and no tool in current analytic or elementary number theory has been shown to surmount that wall for this problem.
