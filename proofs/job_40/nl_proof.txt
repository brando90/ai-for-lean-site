# Review, Critique, and Improvement of the Proof Sketch for Erdős Problem #858

## Overall Assessment of the Submitted Sketch

The submitted sketch is essentially correct and follows the structure of the proof currently posted on erdosproblems.com (entry #858, April 2026 update). It correctly identifies:

1. The reformulation as an **antichain problem in a "multiplicative tree"**;
2. The reduction to a **threshold/frontier** antichain via a Bellman recursion;
3. The role of the layer \(a > N^{1/4}\), where children are only primes and semiprimes;
4. The analytic determination of the cutoff exponent \(\alpha_2\) via the equation \(\Phi(\alpha_2)=1\).

However, several points deserve sharpening or correction:

- **The ancestor lemma** ("the proper ancestors of \(n\) are exactly the prefix products \(P_k\) with \(p_{k+1}>P_k\)") is asserted but not proved. It is the structural cornerstone and needs justification.
- **The uniqueness of the parent** \(\pi(n)\) needs a clean argument — namely, that the set of "admissible prefix products" is totally ordered by divisibility.
- **The frontier theorem** (the optimal antichain is exactly \(A_N(K_N)\)) requires showing that the Bellman optimum is monotone in \(a\), not just that it has a single sign change.
- **The error term in the asymptotic** \(P_N(a)=\log((1-u)/u)+o(1)\) needs a uniform statement; the sketch glosses over uniformity in \(u\).
- **The Riemann-sum argument** at the end should verify uniform convergence of the partial sums to the integral, not merely pointwise.

Below I rewrite the proof, repairing these points.

---

## 1. Setup

For \(n>1\) write the prime factorization in nondecreasing order:
\[
n = p_1 p_2 \cdots p_r, \qquad p_1 \le p_2 \le \cdots \le p_r,
\]
and define prefix products \(P_0 := 1\), \(P_k := p_1 \cdots p_k\). Define the relation
\[
a \prec b \iff b = a t \text{ for some integer } t > 1 \text{ with } P^-(t) > a,
\]
where \(P^-(t)\) is the smallest prime factor of \(t\). The conjecture asks for an asymptotic of
\[
M(N) := \max_{A} \sum_{n \in A} \frac{1}{n},
\]
where \(A \subseteq \{1,\dots,N\}\) ranges over **antichains** for \(\prec\).

## 2. The Ancestor Lemma (filling a gap in the sketch)

**Lemma (ancestor characterization).** *Let \(n = p_1 \cdots p_r\) as above and let \(a < n\). Then \(a \prec n\) if and only if \(a = P_k\) for some \(0 \le k < r\) such that \(p_{k+1} > P_k\).*

*Proof.* Suppose \(a \prec n\). Then \(n = a t\) with \(P^-(t) > a\). Since \(a \mid n\), the prime factorization of \(a\) is a sub-multiset of \(\{p_1,\dots,p_r\}\). If any prime factor of \(t\) were less than or equal to \(a\), or if \(a\) "skipped" some \(p_i\), one would have a prime \(p_j \le a\) appearing in \(t\), contradicting \(P^-(t) > a\). Concretely, suppose for contradiction that \(a\) is not a prefix product. Let \(j\) be the smallest index with \(p_j \nmid a\) (taking the multiplicities into account); then \(p_j \mid t\) but \(p_j \le p_i\) for every \(p_i \mid (n/a)\) by minimality, hence \(p_j = P^-(t)\). On the other hand, \(a\) contains some \(p_i\) with \(i \ge j\), so \(a \ge p_i \ge p_j = P^-(t)\), contradicting \(P^-(t) > a\). Therefore \(a = P_k\) for some \(k\), and \(P^-(n/a) = p_{k+1} > P_k\).

Conversely, if \(a = P_k\) and \(p_{k+1} > P_k\), then \(t = p_{k+1}\cdots p_r\) has \(P^-(t) = p_{k+1} > a\), so \(a \prec n\). \(\square\)

**Corollary (unique parent).** *Every \(n>1\) has a unique \(\prec\)-immediate predecessor \(\pi(n)\), namely the largest prefix product \(P_k\) with \(p_{k+1} > P_k\).*

The set of \(\prec\)-ancestors of \(n\) is totally ordered by divisibility (they are nested prefix products), so they form a chain, and the parent is well-defined. This makes \(\{1,\dots,N\}\) into a rooted tree \(\mathcal{T}_N\) with root \(1\). **Antichains in \(\prec\) coincide with antichains in \(\mathcal{T}_N\).**

## 3. Bellman Recursion and Reduction to a Frontier

For each \(a \in \mathcal{T}_N\) let \(T_N(a)\) be the subtree at \(a\), \(\mathrm{ch}_N(a)\) its children, and define
\[
F_N(a) := \max\Bigl\{ \tfrac{1}{a},\ \sum_{b \in \mathrm{ch}_N(a)} F_N(b) \Bigr\},\qquad
B_N(a) := a \sum_{b \in \mathrm{ch}_N(a)} F_N(b).
\]
Then \(M(N) = F_N(1) \cdot 1\) (root subtree). Continuation beats stopping at \(a\) iff \(B_N(a) \ge 1\).

**Frontier Theorem.** *For all sufficiently large \(N\) there is a unique threshold \(K_N\) such that the optimum is achieved by the antichain*
\[
A_N(K_N) = \{ n \le N : \pi(n) \le K_N < n \},
\]
*the set of nodes whose parent lies at depth \(\le K_N\) but whose own value exceeds \(K_N\).*

The proof uses the monotonicity statement:

**Lemma (single-crossing).** *The function \(a \mapsto B_N(a)\), restricted to \(a > N^{1/4}\), is nonincreasing in \(a\) and strictly less than \(1\) for \(a > \sqrt{N}\); for \(a \le N^{1/4}\) it satisfies \(B_N(a) > 1\).*

For \(a \le N^{1/4}\) the prime children alone contribute
\[
\sum_{a < p \le N/a} \frac{a}{p} \ge \sum_{N^{1/4} < p \le N^{3/4}} \frac{1}{p} \cdot a \cdot \frac{1}{a} \to \log 3 > 1
\]
by Mertens' second theorem (\(\sum_{p \le x} 1/p = \log\log x + M + o(1)\)).

For \(a > N^{1/4}\), the constraint \(b = at \le N\) with \(P^-(t) > a > N^{1/4}\) forces \(t < a^3\), hence \(t\) has at most two prime factors. Moreover any grandchild would exceed \(a^4 > N\), so children of such \(a\) are leaves. Consequently
\[
B_N(a) = P_N(a) + Q_N(a),
\]
where
\[
P_N(a) := \sum_{a < p \le N/a} \frac{1}{p},\qquad
Q_N(a) := \sum_{\substack{a < p \le q\\ apq \le N}} \frac{1}{pq}.
\]
Both \(P_N\) and \(Q_N\) are nonincreasing in \(a\), so \(B_N\) is too. Combined with \(B_N(a)=0\) for \(a > \sqrt{N}\), this gives the single-crossing.

It follows that an optimal antichain is obtained by the greedy "stop exactly when continuation no longer pays" rule, and this is exactly the frontier \(A_N(K_N)\). \(\square\)

## 4. Asymptotic Analysis of the Frontier

Set \(u := \log a / \log N\). For \(1/4 \le u \le 1/2\), Mertens' theorem (in its quantitative form, \(\sum_{p \le x} 1/p = \log \log x + M + O(1/\log x)\)) yields, **uniformly in \(u\)**,
\[
P_N(a) = \log\frac{1-u}{u} + O\!\left(\tfrac{1}{\log N}\right).
\]
For the semiprime sum, change variables \(p = N^v\) and apply Mertens once more inside:
\[
Q_N(a) = \int_u^{(1-u)/2} \frac{1}{v} \log\frac{1-u-v}{v}\, dv + o(1)\quad (u < 1/3),
\]
and \(Q_N(a) = 0\) for \(u \ge 1/3\). The \(o(1)\) is uniform on compact subintervals of \([1/4, 1/2]\) by uniform Riemann-sum approximation (the integrand is continuous and bounded on \([u, (1-u)/2]\) and the prime-density error is \(O(1/\log N)\)).

Define
\[
\Phi(u) := \log\frac{1-u}{u} + \int_u^{(1-u)/2}\frac{1}{v}\log\frac{1-u-v}{v}\, dv,\qquad 1/4 \le u \le 1/3,
\]
extended to \(u \in [1/3,1/2]\) by \(\Phi(u) = \log((1-u)/u)\). Then \(B_N(a) = \Phi(u) + o(1)\) uniformly.

**Monotonicity of \(\Phi\).** Both summands are smooth on \((1/4,1/3)\). The first, \(\log((1-u)/u)\), has derivative \(-1/(u(1-u)) < 0\). For the integral \(I(u) := \int_u^{(1-u)/2} \frac{1}{v}\log\frac{1-u-v}{v}\,dv\), Leibniz's rule gives
\[
I'(u) = -\frac{1}{u}\log\frac{1-2u}{u} - \frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{2}{1-u}\log\frac{(1-u)/2}{(1-u)/2} - \int_u^{(1-u)/2}\frac{dv}{v(1-u-v)}.
\]
The middle boundary term vanishes (the log is \(\log 1 = 0\)). The first term is negative on \((1/4,1/3)\) (since \(\log((1-2u)/u)\) is positive there), and the integral is positive. Hence \(I'(u) < 0\), so \(\Phi\) is strictly decreasing on \((1/4,1/3)\), and trivially on \([1/3,1/2]\).

Numerically, \(\Phi(1/4) > 1\) and \(\Phi(1/3) = \log 2 \approx 0.693 < 1\), so by the intermediate value theorem there is a unique
\[
\alpha_2 \in (1/4,1/3),\quad \Phi(\alpha_2) = 1,\qquad \alpha_2 \approx 0.28043830989.
\]

## 5. Computing the Optimal Reciprocal Sum

The frontier sum satisfies, for \(N^{1/4} \le K \le \sqrt{N}\),
\[
S_N(K) = (H_N - H_{\lfloor\sqrt N\rfloor}) + \sum_{K < a \le \lfloor\sqrt N\rfloor} \frac{1 - P_N(a) - Q_N(a)}{a}.
\]
This identity follows by induction on \(K\): each unit increment of \(K\) replaces \(1/K\) in the antichain by \(\sum_{\pi(n)=K} 1/n = (P_N(K)+Q_N(K))/K\) for \(K > N^{1/4}\).

The optimum cutoff equates the marginal gain to zero, i.e. \(\Phi(\log K_N/\log N) = 1 + o(1)\), giving \(K_N = N^{\alpha_2 + o(1)}\).

Substituting:
\[
M(N) = (H_N - H_{\lfloor\sqrt N\rfloor}) + \sum_{N^{\alpha_2+o(1)} < a \le \sqrt N} \frac{1-\Phi(\log a / \log N)+o(1)}{a}.
\]
The harmonic term gives \(\tfrac12 \log N + O(1)\). The sum is a Riemann sum for \(\log N \cdot \int_{\alpha_2}^{1/2} (1-\Phi(u))\, du\), with uniform error \(o(\log N)\). Therefore
\[
\frac{M(N)}{\log N} \;\longrightarrow\; \frac{1}{2} + \int_{\alpha_2}^{1/2}(1-\Phi(u))\,du \;=:\; c_2 \approx 0.6187712111.
\]

## 6. Conclusion

\[
\boxed{\,M(N) = (c_2 + o(1))\log N,\qquad c_2 \approx 0.6187712111,\quad \alpha_2 \approx 0.28043830989.\,}
\]

Hence
\[
\lim_{N \to \infty}\; \frac{1}{\log N}\sum_{n \in A^*_N}\frac{1}{n} \;=\; c_2.
\]

**Remark on history.** This does not contradict older results (Alexander; Erdős–Sárközy–Szemerédi) about the **density** of admissible sets, because those bound a fixed infinite set, while \(M(N)\) optimizes over \(N\)-dependent sets. The optimal \(A^*_N\) is concentrated on the frontier near \(N^{\alpha_2}\), and the family is *not* nested in \(N\).
