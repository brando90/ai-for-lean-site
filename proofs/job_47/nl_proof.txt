# Smoothed Complexity of the Simplex Method — Proof Sketch (Review and Improvement)

## 1. Status of the Conjecture

The conjecture asks whether some deterministic pivot rule $R$ achieves
$$
S_m^R(m,n,\sigma) \;\le\; O\!\left(n\cdot \operatorname{polylog}(m,n,1/\sigma)\right)
$$
uniformly in $m\ge n$ and $\sigma\in(0,1]$, and more broadly what the asymptotic order of $\inf_R S_m^R(m,n,\sigma)$ is.

After examining the state of the art in smoothed analysis of the simplex method (Spielman–Teng 2004; Vershynin 2009; Dadush–Huiberts 2018; Huiberts–Lee–Zhang, *TheoretiCS* 2025; Bach–Huiberts, arXiv:2504.04197, 2025), I conclude that **the conjecture is genuinely open**, and indeed the existing lower bounds suggest the *literal* near-linear form $O(n\operatorname{polylog}(m,n,1/\sigma))$ may even be **false** for any shadow-vertex–type rule. The previous sketch is largely correct but understates this last point. I revise and expand it below.

## 2. Setup and Almost-Sure Nondegeneracy

Fix the smoothed model: $A=\bar A+G$, $b=\bar b+h$, $c=\bar c+g$, with i.i.d. $\mathcal N(0,\sigma^2)$ entries and $\|(\bar A,\bar b,\bar c)\|\le 1$. Because the Lebesgue density on $(A,b,c)$ is bounded, every algebraic event of positive codimension (e.g. "$d{+}1$ constraints meet," "objective parallel to an edge") has probability zero. Hence with probability one the LP is nondegenerate, every basis is uniquely optimal, and the pivot count $T_R(A,b,c)$ is well defined for any deterministic rule $R$.

In the rest of the proof I will write $d=n$ (variables) and $N=m$ (constraints), to match the literature.

## 3. The Shadow-Vertex Reduction

The unique tool that has produced any polynomial smoothed bounds is the **shadow-vertex pivot rule**. Given a "start" objective $u$ and "target" objective $v$, project $P=\{x: Ax\le b\}$ orthogonally onto $W=\operatorname{span}(u,v)\subseteq\mathbb R^d$. The image $\pi_W(P)$ is a (possibly unbounded) convex polygon. By a standard duality argument, the vertices visited by the shadow rule going from $\arg\max_P u^\top x$ to $\arg\max_P v^\top x$ are in bijection with a subset of the vertices of $\pi_W(P)$. Therefore
$$
T_R(A,b,c) \;\le\; |\operatorname{Vert}(\pi_W(P))|.
$$
The smoothed analysis program then reduces to bounding
$$
\Phi(d,N,\sigma) \;:=\; \sup_{\|(\bar A,\bar b)\|\le 1}\;\sup_{\substack{u,v\in\mathbb R^d\\\text{measurable in }(A,b)}}\;\mathbb E\bigl[|\operatorname{Vert}(\pi_W(P))|\bigr].
$$

A Phase-I trick (e.g. Spielman–Teng's "big-M" or Vershynin's auxiliary LP) bounds initialization by another shadow-path; combined with the optimization shadow path one gets
$$
S_m^{\text{shadow}}(m,n,\sigma) \;\le\; O(\Phi(d,N,\sigma)).
$$

## 4. The Key Geometric Estimates

The estimates underlying every modern bound on $\Phi$ are anti-concentration estimates for Gaussian-perturbed half-space arrangements.

**Lemma (Slack lower bound).** For a vertex $v$ of $P$ defined by a basis $B\subset[N]$ of size $d$, and any non-basis index $i\notin B$, the slack $b_i - a_i^\top v$ has, conditional on $B$ being the active basis, a density bounded by $O(1/\sigma)$ in a neighborhood of $0$. Consequently
$$
\Pr[\,b_i - a_i^\top v \in [0,t]\,] \;\le\; O(t/\sigma).
$$

**Lemma (Angle / edge-length trade-off).** Two consecutive vertices $v_k, v_{k+1}$ of $\pi_W(P)$ either bound an edge of length $\ge \ell$, or generate an exterior angle $\ge \theta$, where $\ell\theta \gtrsim$ slack. After Gaussian smoothing, with overwhelming probability a controlled fraction of consecutive shadow edges have lengths $\ge 1/\operatorname{poly}(d,N,1/\sigma)$.

Summing edge-lengths and exterior angles around the closed polygon $\pi_W(P)$ gives total perimeter $\le \operatorname{poly}(\sigma^{-1})\cdot\sqrt{\log N}$ and total exterior angle $\le 2\pi$, hence at most $\operatorname{poly}(d,1/\sigma)\cdot \operatorname{polylog}(N)$ vertices in expectation.

## 5. Best Known Quantitative Bounds (2025)

A careful execution of the program above yields the **Huiberts–Lee–Zhang (TheoretiCS 2025)** bound
$$
\Phi(d,N,\sigma) \;\le\; O\!\bigl(\sigma^{-3/2}\, d^{13/4}\, \log^{7/4} N\bigr),
$$
and the further **Bach–Huiberts (2025)** improvement, which uses an *auxiliary random objective* $z$ to break adversarial alignments:
$$
\Phi(d,N,\sigma) \;\le\; O\!\bigl(\sigma^{-1/2}\, d^{11/4}\, \log^{7/4} N\bigr).
$$
Both papers handle initialization by a Phase-I shadow path, so the same bound (up to constants) holds for the *full* simplex pivot count $S_m^R$ when $R$ is the corresponding (auxiliary-objective) shadow-vertex rule.

These bounds have:

- **polylogarithmic** dependence on $N$, the number of constraints — so the conjecture's $\operatorname{polylog}(m)$ requirement *is* met by shadow-vertex methods;
- **polynomial** dependence on $d=n$, currently $d^{11/4}$, far worse than linear;
- **polynomial** dependence on $1/\sigma$, currently $\sigma^{-1/2}$, while the conjecture demands only $\operatorname{polylog}(1/\sigma)$.

So the conjecture is *not* established for any pivot rule.

## 6. Lower Bounds: Why the Conjecture Is Likely False (at least for shadow rules, and plausibly in general)

The previous sketch only mentioned lower bounds in passing. They are decisive in narrowing the picture:

1. **Huiberts–Lee–Zhang (2025)** prove a smoothed lower bound for any shadow-vertex pivot rule of the form
$$
\mathbb E[T_R] \;\ge\; \Omega\!\bigl(\sigma^{-1/2}\sqrt{d}\bigr)
$$
in the regime $N\gg d$. This already rules out near-linear in $d$ with polylogarithmic-in-$1/\sigma$ behavior for shadow-vertex rules.

2. **Bach–Huiberts (2025)** prove a high-probability *diameter* lower bound for smoothed polytopes:
$$
\operatorname{diam}(P) \;\ge\; \Omega\!\bigl(\sigma^{-1/2}\, d^{1/2}\,(\log(4/\sigma))^{-1/4}\bigr)
$$
for $N$ exponentially large in $d$. Since *any* pivot rule that produces a monotone path between two vertices is bounded below by the (monotone) diameter, this rules out
$$
\inf_R S_m^R(m,n,\sigma)\le O(n\operatorname{polylog}(m,n,1/\sigma))
$$
in the regime where $\sigma$ is small compared to $1$ and $m$ is super-polynomial in $n$. Concretely, $\sigma^{-1/2}$ cannot be replaced by $\operatorname{polylog}(1/\sigma)$.

Combining (2) with the conjecture's quantifiers ("uniformly for all $m\ge n$ and $\sigma\in(0,1]$") yields:

> **Proposition (consequence of Bach–Huiberts).** *There exists $c>0$ such that for every pivot rule $R$, every sufficiently small $\sigma$, and $m=2^{cn}$,*
> $$
> S_m^R(m,n,\sigma) \;\ge\; \Omega\!\bigl(\sigma^{-1/2}\sqrt{n}\bigr).
> $$
> *In particular, no pivot rule satisfies the bound $S_m^R(m,n,\sigma)\le O(n\operatorname{polylog}(m,n,1/\sigma))$ uniformly in $\sigma\in(0,1]$ and $m\ge n$.*

So under the standard reading of the conjecture, the stated explicit bound is in fact **false**, and the conjecture (in the affirmative direction) is refuted as written. The genuinely open question is the *correct asymptotic order* of $\inf_R S_m^R$, where the current best bracket is approximately
$$
\Omega\!\bigl(\sigma^{-1/2}\sqrt d\bigr) \;\le\; \inf_R S_m^R(m,n,\sigma) \;\le\; O\!\bigl(\sigma^{-1/2} d^{11/4}\log^{7/4} m\bigr),
$$
with the lower bound holding for $m$ exponentially large in $n$ and the upper bound holding uniformly.

## 7. Critique and Improvements over the Previous Sketch

The earlier sketch is structurally correct but I recommend three corrections and additions:

1. **It conflates "open" with "unproven."** The Bach–Huiberts diameter lower bound essentially refutes the explicit bound $O(n\operatorname{polylog}(m,n,1/\sigma))$ when one allows $m$ exponential in $n$ and $\sigma\to 0$. The verdict should reflect that the literal conjecture is most likely **false**, while the broader question of the asymptotic order remains open.

2. **It states the slack/anti-concentration step too informally.** The crucial probabilistic input is not just "small slacks are unlikely" but a quantitative density bound $O(1/\sigma)$ on the slack distribution at a fixed vertex, which is what controls the polynomial dependence on $\sigma$. This is the step that one would need to improve to remove the $\sigma^{-1/2}$ factor — and the Bach–Huiberts diameter lower bound shows such improvement is impossible in general.

3. **It overlooks the role of the auxiliary random objective.** This is not merely "the main reason $\sigma$-dependence improves"; it is what allows shadow-vertex analysis to *avoid* worst-case orientations of the projection plane $W$. Without it, the dependence is even worse. This should be highlighted as the technical core of the 2025 improvements.

4. **Initialization is not a side remark.** The Phase-I LP can dominate the pivot count if not handled carefully; in modern proofs, the "interpolation" Phase-I construction (Vershynin / Dadush–Huiberts) is itself a smoothed shadow path, requiring the same anti-concentration toolkit.

## 8. What a Resolution Would Need

Either:

- **(Affirmative resolution, ruled out as stated.)** Improving the upper bound to $O(n\operatorname{polylog}(m,n,1/\sigma))$ would require a new pivot rule that is *not* a shadow rule (since shadow rules have an $\Omega(\sigma^{-1/2}\sqrt d)$ smoothed lower bound), and would have to circumvent the diameter lower bound — impossible when $m=2^{\Omega(n)}$.

- **(Refined positive result, possibly tractable.)** A near-linear bound of the form $O(n\operatorname{polylog}(m,n))\cdot f(\sigma)$ with $f(\sigma)$ polynomial in $\sigma^{-1}$ might be achievable; matching the lower bound $\sigma^{-1/2}\sqrt d$ would be the natural target.

- **(Negative resolution, the most likely outcome.)** Proving a stronger lower bound, e.g. $\inf_R S_m^R(m,n,\sigma)\ge \Omega(\sigma^{-1/2} d)$ or stronger, would close the gap from below.

## Final Verdict

VERDICT: OPEN — the broader asymptotic question $\inf_R S_m^R(m,n,\sigma)$ is unresolved (best known bracket roughly $\sigma^{-1/2}\sqrt n \le \inf_R S_m^R \le \sigma^{-1/2} n^{11/4}\log^{7/4}m$), and although the *literal* near-linear bound $O(n\operatorname{polylog}(m,n,1/\sigma))$ appears to be ruled out by the Bach–Huiberts smoothed diameter lower bound when $m$ is exponentially large in $n$, no published theorem formally settles the conjecture as stated.
